Three Questions About Parlay Pricing — Quick Answers

Q1 and Q2 share a root cause: percentage points (pp) and ROI percent are different units. Q3 walks through how I compute fair value.

Q1: "The chart shows tiny margins on longshots — but I thought books make the most on long odds. Is the chart wrong?"

Both observations are true. The chart shows absolute pp, not per-dollar edge. Long odds have small pp gaps but huge per-dollar edge — that's where the longshot profit lives.
Short Answer
A 1pp markup at +1900 odds is a 17% edge per dollar wagered. The same 1pp at +120 is only 2.4%. The chart plots pp; "books love longshots" lives in the proportional version. Both are true at once.

Same 1pp markup, vastly different per-dollar edge. A flat 2% vig collects 17% per $ wagered on +1900 longshots and 2% on coinflips. That longshot profit is hidden when the chart is plotted in pp.

Why does the chart show pp growing with fair prob? Because vig is multiplicative, not additive. offered = fair × (1 + vig) means at fair=5% the gap is 0.1pp, at fair=40% the gap is 0.8pp. Geometry, not pricing policy.

Q2: "Your pp-distance averages 1.5pp but your realized ROI is ~3.75%. Are you just lucky in the short run?"

No — converting pp to ROI% requires dividing by offered probability. 1.5pp on the chart implies a per-dollar edge of ~3-6% depending on the prob mix. ROI of 3.75% is consistent with the pricing, not lucky outperformance.
Short Answer
Same root cause as Q1 — pp ≠ ROI%. Conversion: edge% = pp_gap ÷ offered_prob. A 1.5pp markup at fair=30% (typical 3-leg parlay) maps to 4.8% edge per $ wagered. Across the full mix of parlays I quote, implied per-dollar edge averages 3-6%. Realized 3.75% sits inside that range — not luck, the pp framing was understating the true edge.

Realized ROI of 3.75% on risked volume sits inside the expected band. The chart's pp reading didn't predict 1.5% — it predicted 3-6% depending on which fair-prob bucket dominates fill volume. The apparent gap was a unit-mismatch artifact, not luck.

Caveat: variance is real. A true 5% edge can produce realized ROI between 0% and 8% over a few hundred fills. The pp-to-edge% conversion settles the apparent 1.5% vs 3.75% gap; sample-size variance ultimately decides whether 3.75% is the long-run number.

Q3: "How do you de-vig? How are your fair values computed?"

Five-step pipeline per leg: collect each book's two-sided implied probs → drop high-vig outliers → de-vig each book → average the survivors → floor at Pinnacle on favorites. For a parlay, multiply leg fair probs (with correlation adjustments for same-game combos).
StepWhat HappensWhy
1 Collect 2-sided book pairs. For a moneyline, pull each book's home + away American odds and convert to implied probs. Vig only shows up when both sides are quoted. One-sided markets carry no signal to remove.
2 Drop high-vig books. Any book whose two-side implied probs sum above 106% is excluded from the consensus. Pinnacle ~102%, DK/FD 104-105%. Books above 110% (Asian square-bookmaking feeds, retail outliers) drag the consensus toward worse-than-fair pricing.
3 De-vig each surviving book using fair = imp / (imp_home + imp_away) — proportional redistribution of the overround. Standard method. Each book yields one (fair_home, fair_away) pair summing to 1.0.
4 Average the per-book fairs. Simple mean across surviving books → consensus fair prob. Multiple sharp books cross-validate each other; one alone is too noisy.
5 Floor at Pinnacle on favorites. For any side with consensus fair ≥ 50%, take max(consensus, Pinnacle's de-vigged fair). Pinnacle is the sharpest, lowest-vig book. The floor prevents the consensus from drifting cheaper than Pin on a favorite due to residual noise.

Home leg fair = 63.6%. We add our own vig on top to produce the offered price.

Multi-Leg Parlay: the parlay fair prob is the product of leg fair probs.

parlay_fair = leg1.fair × leg2.fair × leg3.fair × ...

Three legs at 63.6% / 50% / 55% compound to 0.636 × 0.50 × 0.55 = 17.5% parlay fair. That's the "Fair" value on the chart and the basis for the offered price.

Two Adjustments Before The Multiply:

  • SGP correlation. Two legs on the same game aren't independent — the naive product understates positively-correlated joint probs (favorite + over) and overstates negative ones (favorite + under). The joint prob is multiplied by an empirically-calibrated factor (1.15 positive / 0.90 negative). Same approach books use internally.
  • Calibration backstop. If a totals-type leg's de-vigged fair drifts more than ±10pp from a simpler book-consensus mean (Pin/DK/FD averaged), the leg auto-declines as an "anomalous total" — a safety net for feed-shape edge cases.

Special-Case Methods Used Elsewhere:

  • Additive-margin de-vig with a 0.5 favShare cap. Used for the DraftKings golf scraper and the manual Bookmaker upload. Pure proportional de-vig over-corrects heavy favorites (on −3000/+1300 it strips ~4pp off the favorite); the capped method limits each side to absorbing at most half the overround, keeping fair closer to what the book actually posts.
  • Naive 2-way de-vig. Used for golf matchup pricing where only DataGolf's two probabilities are available — same imp / (imp1 + imp2) formula, single source.
TL;DR — fair value isn't a model; it's a consensus de-vig of multiple sharp books, with Pinnacle as a floor on favorites. Per-leg fair → parlay fair via product (with SGP correlation factors). Then I add my own vig to that for the offered price.
TL;DR for Q1/Q2 — pp is additive on probability; ROI is multiplicative on dollars. The chart's pp framing makes longshot margins look small and overall edge look low — neither is what's actually happening with the dollars. Divide pp by offered prob to get the dollar edge, and both apparent paradoxes resolve.
