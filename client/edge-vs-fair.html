Distribution shape

Reading the scatter:

  • Long-tail parlays (low fair prob, left side) show the biggest edges — 5–9 leg parlays clustering 8–25% per-$. That's the leg-count vig ramp (VIG_BY_LEG_COUNT) doing its job: charging a premium for variance the bettor is asking us to absorb.
  • Near-50/50 parlays (right side) sit closer to the break-even line. Most are still positive but thin — these are mostly 2-leg moneyline / total combos where there's no stack-of-favorites surcharge to charge.
  • Negative-edge cluster at fair ≈ 22–34% is the worth-investigating cohort. Pin a few of those to see which combos — they're prime candidates for stale-price slippage, missed correlation, or template-ramp underpricing on accounts that hammer the same template repeatedly.
  • Big bubble at fair ≈ 32%, edge ≈ 1.8% = the $1,304 confirmed parlay (Detroit Pistons ML + Seattle Mariners ML). Largest ticket of the period, but not the highest-edge — a 2-leg, so no leg-count premium.

Realized P&L flip

Realized P&L flipped from +$1,213 last week to −$266 this week, off 66 settled parlays. This is variance, not a pricing-quality signal. On 66 parlays at ~$200 average stake and 4.8% theoretical edge, one-σ swing is ~$3k+. A move from +$1,213 to −$266 sits well inside one σ.

Why we trust theoretical over realized at this sample size
On 60-ish settled parlays per ~33h, realized P&L is dominated by which side of the variance curve you happened to land on, not by how sharp the prices were. The theoretical edge metric is calibrated at quote-time against book-derived fair value — it tells you what the prices should earn long-run. As long as that stays positive and stable, realized P&L will mean-revert there over hundreds of parlays. One-window realized P&L on small samples should not drive vig-curve changes.
